# Playbook — Churn response

Apply when a customer hits **at-risk** status (any of: churn-risk score >
0.5, exec sponsor turnover, usage decline > 15% QoQ).

## Day 0 — Within 48 hours of flagging

1. **Schedule a renewal call** with the customer's new
   procurement/economic buyer. Don't wait for them to ask.
2. **Identify a replacement exec sponsor** if the original departed.
   Without a sponsor, you're already lost.
3. **Pull the usage report** for the last 90 days and bring it to the
   call. The customer's own data is more persuasive than yours.

## Week 1 — Diagnose

- What changed in the customer's business that drove usage down?
  (Layoffs? Product pivot? Team restructure?)
- What's the *replacement* — did they consolidate onto a competitor,
  or are they doing without?
- Is the contract structurally wrong (e.g. seat count way too high)?
  Right-sizing is often the rescue.

## Week 2-4 — Pitch the save

Three flavors, ranked:

1. **Right-size + multi-year** — drop seats to actual usage, lock a
   2-year term at the right-sized price. Sticky, predictable, gives them
   a budget win.
2. **Pause the upcoming auto-renewal**, switch to month-to-month for 90
   days while they evaluate. Buys you time to deepen the relationship.
3. **Negotiated downgrade** — move from a higher tier to a lower one
   with a clean exit if usage doesn't recover by next renewal.

## Don't

- ❌ Don't lead with discounts on the existing contract — sets the
  precedent that price was the issue when it usually wasn't
- ❌ Don't escalate to the buyer's boss without sponsor permission —
  burns the relationship
- ❌ Don't make the renewal call the *first* time the customer hears
  about the at-risk flag

## When to declare lost

If you can't get the renewal call scheduled within 30 days, you've
probably already lost. Move the deal to forecast as churn and start
preparing the offboarding handoff so the customer experience is clean.
